How to Play

You lead a nation for 10 years. Each year you pick one policy, face a random event, then advance to the next year. Your choices shape four national stats — manage them all to avoid collapse and build a sustainable future.

🎯 Objective
🌿Best outcome

Reach Year 10 with Emissions ≤ 25 and Green Progress ≥ 65 to achieve the Green Success ending — a fully sustainable nation.

⚖️Good outcome

Reach Year 10 with Economy ≥ 70 and Emissions ≤ 45 for Balanced Reform — strong growth on a cleaner path.

💀Instant loss

If Economy, Approval, or Emissions hit their limit (0 or 100) at any point, the game ends immediately — no waiting for Year 10.

🔁Turn structure

Each year: (1) hover policies to preview effects → (2) click one to apply it → (3) a random event may strike → (4) advance the year.

📊 The Four Stats
StatRangeWhat it means & danger zone
💰 Economy 0 – 100 Represents jobs, investment, and national stability. Hitting 0 triggers instant Economic Collapse. Keep it above 20 — the red danger zone. Some clean policies hurt short-term growth, so plan ahead.
💨 CO₂ Emissions 0 – 100 Your nation's carbon output. Hitting 100 triggers instant Climate Disaster. This is the hardest stat to recover — small reductions each year compound over time. Emissions also feed the Climate Risk meter.
📣 Public Approval 0 – 100 Your political support. Hitting 0 triggers instant Political Collapse. Protests, unpopular taxes, and economic pain all drain it. Disaster relief and popular green programs rebuild it.
🌿 Green Progress 0 – 100 Your nation's overall sustainability investment. This doesn't cause a direct loss, but low green progress locks you out of the best endings and leaves you vulnerable to climate events.
🔥 Climate Risk Meter
📈It only goes up

Unlike Emissions, Climate Risk accumulates permanently. Even if you cut emissions dramatically, risk already built up stays. Think of it as locked-in damage from years of high carbon output.

⚡Risk amplifies events

At Risk ≥ 50, negative events deal 25% more damage. At Risk ≥ 75, damage increases to 50% more. Events also trigger more frequently at high risk.

🌡️What raises it

Every year that passes with high emissions adds passive risk. Choosing fossil fuel expansion accelerates it. Certain events (wildfires, floods) add a small bonus risk hit on top.

💡The lesson

This mechanic models climate tipping points — the idea that early action matters far more than late-stage fixes. Delay now means compounding consequences you can't undo.

🔁 Policy Cooldowns
1️⃣First use — full effect

Every policy starts at full strength. The numbers on the card are accurate for the first time you pick it.

2️⃣Second use — 75% effect

The second time you use the same policy, all its effects are scaled to 75%. A yellow badge appears on the card to warn you.

3️⃣Third+ use — 50% effect

After two prior uses, the policy runs at 50% effectiveness. The badge turns red. You can still use it, but returns are diminishing.

🎯Why it exists

Real climate action can't rely on a single repeated solution. Cooldowns force you to build a diverse policy mix across all 10 years.

⚡ Policy Synergy Combos
🔗How combos work

When you pick 2 or more consecutive clean or regulation policies, you trigger a synergy bonus: +3 Green Progress and −2 Emissions are added on top of the policy's normal effects.

✅What counts as "clean"

Renewable Energy, Green Jobs, Public Transit, Environmental Regulations, and Carbon Tax all count. Fossil Fuel, Delay, and Disaster Relief break the streak.

💥Breaking the streak

Picking Fossil Fuel expansion or Delay resets your combo to 0. You'll need two consecutive clean picks again before the bonus activates.

🏆Tracked in analytics

Your ending screen shows how many times you triggered a synergy combo — a mark of advanced play.

🎲 Random Events

After each policy decision, there is a chance a random event strikes. Events are weighted by your current stats — high emissions makes disasters more likely; high green progress makes positive events more likely. Events also trigger more often and hit harder at high Climate Risk.

💡 Strategy Tips
1

Hover before you click. The tooltip on each policy card shows exactly what your stats will look like after the change. Use it every turn — surprises are bad.

2

Start green, stay green. Emissions are very hard to reduce in the late game once Climate Risk is high. Cut early, cut often. The first 3–4 years set the trajectory for everything.

3

Diversify your policies. Cooldowns punish repeating the same choice. Use all 8 policies across 10 years — the game rewards variety and penalizes single-track thinking.

4

Chain clean policies for combo bonuses. Two consecutive clean picks give +3 Green and −2 Emissions free. That's 30 bonus green and −20 bonus emissions if you keep the chain all game.

5

Watch your approval floor. Once approval drops below 40 you start seeing protest events that drain it further. Use Disaster Relief or Green Jobs to stop the slide before it becomes a spiral.

6

Climate Risk is the hidden timer. It makes bad events more common and more punishing. A run where you stay under 40 risk feels completely different from one where risk is 70+.

7

Save relief for emergencies. Disaster Relief Spending is the most powerful approval tool. Don't waste it on routine dips — hold it for when a brutal event chain or approval drops below 30.

8

The ending screen tells you why you lost. Check the analytics — it shows your playstyle, your most used policy, your biggest weakness, and your worst event. Use that to play smarter next run.

About the Stats

Economy (0–100) represents jobs, investment, and national stability. Hitting 0 means collapse.

Emissions (0–100) measures your country's carbon output. Hitting 100 triggers a climate disaster.

Approval (0–100) is your political support. Fall to 0 and you lose power.

Green Progress tracks your overall sustainability investment over time.

What This Game Demonstrates

💰

Economy = Livelihoods

Economic stats represent jobs, wages, and stability. Green transitions have real costs — and real benefits — for working people.

🌫️

Emissions = Real Harm

Carbon emissions represent environmental damage that accumulates. Delayed action compounds consequences over time.

🗳️

Approval = Political Reality

Even the best policy fails without public support. Leaders must communicate, build coalitions, and bring people along.

🌍

It's Systemic

Climate change is driven by policy, corporate power, and political trade-offs — not just individual choices. Systems must change.

The core lesson: Every policy choice creates winners and losers. There is no perfect solution — only trade-offs. Real climate action requires navigating economic pressures, political opposition, and social equity simultaneously. That is why systemic change is hard, and why it matters.
